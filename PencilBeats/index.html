Fundamental Unit of Life

What is a Cell?

A cell is the smallest structural and functional unit of life that is capable of carrying out all essential biological processes. Every living organism, whether unicellular or multicellular, is made up of cells. The discovery of the cell was made by Robert Hooke in 1665 when he observed a thin slice of cork under a microscope. Cells arise from pre-existing cells and contain hereditary material that is passed from one generation to another. In unicellular organisms like Amoeba, a single cell performs all life functions, whereas in multicellular organisms, cells are specialized to perform specific tasks. Thus, the cell forms the basic building block of life, responsible for structure, growth, metabolism, and reproduction.

Types of Cells

Prokaryotic

Cells are simple, small, and lack a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Their genetic material is present in a region called the nucleoid. Examples include bacteria and cyanobacteria.

Eukaryotic

Cells are larger, more complex, and contain a well-defined nucleus enclosed by a nuclear membrane. They also possess membrane-bound organelles such as mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum, and Golgi apparatus. Eukaryotic cells are found in plants, animals, fungi, and protists.
This classification helps in understanding the evolutionary development of organisms and differences in cellular organization and functions.

Tissues

What is a Tissue?

A tissue is a group of similar cells that work together to perform a particular function. In simple organisms, one cell does everything, but in complex organisms like plants and humans, work is divided among different cells. This division of labour makes the body more efficient and organized.
